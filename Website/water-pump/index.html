Water Pump

Designed, analyzed, and machined a double-cylinder piston pump in aluminum as part of a team of six. Required to pump water 1 meter vertically at a minimum flow rate of 1 L/min.

MAE 2250 — Mechanical Synthesis Team of 6 Cornell University

Project Requirements

This was a structured design-build-test project from MAE 2250 (Mechanical Synthesis). The prompt was deliberately open-ended on implementation but firm on performance: the pump had to lift water 1 meter and sustain at least 1 L/min flow. Everything else — pump type, geometry, materials, sealing approach — was up to the team.

Design

The team selected a double-cylinder reciprocating piston pump — two parallel aluminum cylinders with pistons driven by a shared threaded-rod actuator mechanism. The double-cylinder layout allows one cylinder to be on the intake stroke while the other is on the discharge stroke, producing more continuous flow than a single-cylinder design and reducing pulsation.

Double-cylinder reciprocating piston design

Two machined aluminum cylinders are mounted in parallel and connected by a central actuator assembly. Flat aluminum pistons with O-ring seals displace water on each stroke. The end caps are machined aluminum plates with drilled and tapped ports for inlet and outlet fittings.

CAD in Fusion 360

The full pump assembly was modeled in Fusion 360. This included the cylinder bores, end caps, piston geometry, threaded rod tie structure, and the port placements for inlet/outlet fittings. Tolerances were designed to allow the O-ring seals to function correctly while keeping the pistons free to travel without binding.

CFD Analysis

Computational fluid dynamics simulation in Fusion 360 was used to validate the flow path geometry through the end caps — checking that the port sizing and internal channel geometry would support the required flow rate without excessive pressure drop across the fittings.

FEA

Finite element analysis was run on the high-stress components of the assembly. The scotch yoke — which receives rotational input from the drive shaft and converts it to linear reciprocating motion — was a primary focus. Von Mises stress analysis confirmed the yoke would remain well within the material's yield strength under operating loads, with a peak stress of 2.819 MPa.

Machining & Assembly

All aluminum components were machined in-house. The cylinder bores were turned on a lathe to achieve the tight tolerances required for the O-ring piston seals. End caps were milled and drilled for the bolt pattern and port fittings. Piston discs were turned to match the bore diameter with appropriate clearance for the O-ring grooves.

Assembly required careful sequencing — O-rings seated into the piston grooves, pistons inserted into the cylinders, end caps bolted down with the threaded rod tie structure to compress the assembly. Getting the preload on the tie rods right was important to keep the cylinders sealed at the end caps without distorting the bore geometry.

Project Management

With a team of six and a hard deadline for the live test, project management was a real part of the work — not just a checkbox. The project included:

Deliverables beyond the hardware

  • Bill of Materials (BOM): Full BOM tracking all purchased and machined components, quantities, material specs, and costs
  • Project Schedule: Gantt-style schedule breaking down design, analysis, machining, assembly, and testing phases with assigned owners and milestones
  • Design Review Documentation: Written justification of design decisions, analysis results, and risk assessment ahead of committing to fabrication
  • Test Plan & Results: Defined pass/fail criteria before testing; documented actual measured flow rate and head against requirements

Skills & Tools

  • CAD: Full assembly modeling in Fusion 360, including tolerance analysis for piston/bore fit
  • CFD: Flow simulation in Fusion 360 to validate port geometry and minimize pressure losses
  • FEA: Structural analysis of end caps and cylinder walls under operating pressure
  • Machining: Lathe (turning bores, pistons, cylinder bodies) and mill (end caps, bolt patterns, ports)
  • GD&T: Tolerancing for O-ring piston seals and cylinder bore fits
  • Project Management: BOM, schedule, design reviews, and test documentation across a team of six
