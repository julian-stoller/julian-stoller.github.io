FFF vs SLA Printing Study

MAE 4450 Cornell University Spring 2026 Additive Manufacturing FFF / FDM SLA Resin Metrology Coursework Team Project

Overview

For Project #1 in MAE 4450 (Additive Manufacturing Process Physics, Materials, Properties, and Post Processing), my team designed a gear-based snap-fit fidget toy and printed it with two additive manufacturing processes to compare their dimensional accuracy, surface finish, and assembly behavior.

The FFF parts were printed on a Bambu Lab X1C (0.4 mm nozzle, PLA, 0.20 mm layers, 15% infill, no supports; 47 min print). The SLA parts were printed on a Formlabs Form 3 (Grey Pro V1 resin, 0.050 mm layers, solid infill, fine-tip supports; 3 hr print). Five critical dimensions — gear tooth widths, the large gear track interior and exterior diameters, and the track carrier length — were measured five times each with digital calipers to quantify each process's repeatability and deviation from the CAD model.

This was a group project completed with Mattan Kelchner and Mae Sliwinski.

Key Findings

FFF produced a more forgiving snap-fit (its peg-hole behaved like a clearance fit) and required minimal post-processing, but it rounded the tooth corners and showed first-layer warping from poor bed adhesion. SLA produced sharper, better-defined teeth but shrank tighter-than-intended mating features, and support removal made its thin connecting arms brittle enough to break during assembly.

A file-scaling error (the SLA parts were printed at 70% scale) was caught after printing, so a parity-scaled comparison was used: once corrected, the two processes agreed to within 0.4–2.5% on most features, confirming that the large circular features were highly repeatable while the small tooth geometry was the most process-sensitive.

The report also develops a mathematical model for FFF print time as a function of layer height, fitting a representative per-layer time of 27.4 s via least-squares regression against slicer data and accounting for a constant ~16.8 min startup time.

FFF (Bambu X1C, PLA) SLA (Form 3, Grey Pro)
Tooth definition Rounded corners Sharp, well-defined
Peg-hole fit Clearance (rotates freely) Interference (press fit)
Dimensional trend Slightly oversized / expands Slightly undersized / shrinks
Post-processing Minimal (no supports) Wash + support removal
Print time 47 min 3 hr

The team concluded that SLA is preferable when visual quality and fine feature definition matter most, while FFF is the more viable choice for a durable, repeatedly-handled consumer part.
